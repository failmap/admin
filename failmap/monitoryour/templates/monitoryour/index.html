Example: The Netherlands

How to get thousands of vulnerabilities fixed?

The Kingdom of the Netherlands is devided into many municipalities. They are responsible for a lot of privacy sensitive data: who lives there, welfare programmes, childcare and more.

Dutch municipalities have about 5000 websites and a ton of other services. Each of which increases the attack surface: the amount of locations things can go wrong.

Decades of security tests, auditors and self regulation has resulted in bad security at the fundamental level. Even with best efforts and best intentions, the most well willing organizations fail to deliver in the most basic security categories. This needs to change.

Transparency increases security

We've changed their IT by creating transparency about the state of their most basic security. This led to insight for everyone, and as a result thousands of vulnerabilities fixed!

Without increasing risk, you too can push the government into the right direction. Start monitoring yourself using our open source community driven approach: failmap.

What is being monitored?

What will be monitored

Failmap monitors a variety of modern security standards that are the absolute basis for any application and organization that handles sensitive information. Many of these are requirements for governments in Europe and the United States, others are common sense security requirements that are well documented by respected organizations.

Currently monitoring is aimed at web technologies on the public internet. For example Transport Layer Security (TLS), which encrypts data. Encryption guarantees that nobody can eavesdrop or manipulate data when in transit. This ensures privacy and integrity.

With seven technologies monitored, we've seen a giant uptake in the quality of service provided with our scans of the Netherlands. Imagine what will happen when even more technologies are added!

Monitoring scope and balance

When creating transparency in security, there is a trade off between what is being made public and what isn't. We've carefully balanced this and came to the list of technologies that create the lowest bar in information security.

This lowest bar is described on the section on the left: each of those subject can be scanned using dozens of online security scanners. Many of these security requirements are also mandated by governments, yet (still) not implemented.

Security and privacy are very important to us, therefore nothing is published that severly increases risks. Examples of things not being monitored are vulnerabilities that might leak large amounts of data. Those types of vulnerabilities need to be disclosed exclusively to the organization that hosts the service, using Responsible Disclosure.

Not monitored

  • SQL Injection

  • Weak credentials
  • Services without credentials (e.g. VNC)
  • Other severe vulnerabilities

Open source monitoring software

Failmap is an open source project, which means you can see how it works and even change it if needed. We release under A-GPL, which insures that any changes will have to be shared with the community too!

Installation

System requirements

Failmap can be run on a dedicated Virtual Machine. We recommend:

  • OS: Debian Jesse and Stretch (versions 8 and 9).
  • Ram: 4GB
  • Disk: 40GB
  • Cores: 2 or more

A VM with these specs is available around 20 euro/month and cheaper.

Installation

The installation procedure is simlpe and straightforward. You need a server, installed with Debian, sudo permissions and SSH access. Deploy from any Mac or Linux workstation, with Puppet and Git.

Installation instructions are listed here:
https://gitlab.com/failmap/server/blob/master/documentation/hosting.md

Installation Example

Operations

Failmap comes with several commands to automatically upgrade and manage your installation.

For example: to update to the latest version of failmap, just login over SSH and run:

failmap-deploy

and follow the instructions.

Operation commands are listed here:
https://gitlab.com/failmap/server/blob/master/documentation/operations.md

Frequently Asked Questions

What can i expect when monitoring?

Our story: We started this project in 2016, with just checking for TLS weaknesses. This was presented in a crows that had a sizeable amount of Chief Security Officers of Dutch Municipalities. The visualization of the particulary bad state of security sparked some panic in the room and people started making calls. One weekend later 150 TLS vulnerabilites where fixed.

We've continued our journey to better the infrastructure, creating an open source monitoring tool and ever increasing the amount of services and technologies we scan. We've been awarded a Potentials Grant by the SIDN Foundation to make sure this software will exist for years to come.

The reception has been largely positive, with 1 complaint on about every 100 support requests we get. We're still receiving mails every day asking for re-scans, advice and more. Municipalities in the Netherlands are very professional organizations and now we have proof that they are serious about security whatever they connect to the internet.

With your approach we've solved thousands of vulnerabilities, making the internet a safer place for Dutch citizens.

-- Elger Jonker, Chair of the Internet Cleanup Foundation.

Other questions

Is monitoring legal?

Similar services have existed for years, albeit without a nice map interface. For example: all major security vendors supply online scanners to check your website. Qualys has SSL Labs, Verisign allows you to check DNSSEC.

If you live in a country with a repressive, backwards, government, trying to increase their IT security is a poor choice. In that case, do NOT deploy this software.

We're not lawyers and it might be worthwhile to check with your national Digital Rights activists.

The goal is to create a better internet. This is a very effective and safe way to do so.
